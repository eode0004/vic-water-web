Water in Victoria

Life's most precious resource

Introduction

Out of all the resources harvested and distributed across Victoria, water is undoubtedly the most vital. With it, crops grow, industry thrives, and ecosystems flourish; without it, life as we know it becomes impossible. This dashboard aims to provide an overview of water in Victoria, in hopes of highlighting the incredible magnitude and importance of what is done with this resource in our state.

Water Metrics of Victoria

Where water is located is only half the picture; it is equally important to know how much water is actually flowing through out state. The map below shows this alongside the temperatures and turbidities of water at select points across the state. Our waterways naturally vary over time; as such, you can choose to view how the clarity, volume, and temperature of water changed across the span of 2024-2025.

Turbidity is a measure of how clear water is; here, it is measured in Nephelometric Turbidity Units (NTU), which are based on how much light is scattered by particles suspended in the water. The higher the NTU, the murkier the water. For reference, drinking water typically has an NTU of less than 1, while stormwater can have an NTU of 100 or more.

Fun fact: Streamflow (i.e. how much water flows through a section of river) per day is measured in megalitres. An olympic swimming pool contains about 2.5 megalitres of water, so if a river has a streamflow of 500 megalitres per day, that means about 200 Olympic swimming pools worth of water is flowing through it every day!

Water Entitlements and Availability

Of course, not all water in Victoria is available for use, and even less of that is actually entitled to water users and distributors. Below, it can be seen both how much water is available and how much constitutes water entitlements on a yearly basis. Often, much more water is available than is actually entitled; this helps to account for the variability in the amount of available water, allowing entitlement to remain consistent (as can be seen below).

These charts (and others later on) measure water in gigalitres (GL), which represent a thousand megalitres. One gigalitre is enough water to fill 400 Olympic swimming pools, and though this is already a staggering quantity of water, the charts below demonstrate that thousands of gigalitres of water are entitled for use, and often tens of thousands are made available in the course of a year.
